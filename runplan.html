Overview

While training for the Amsterdam Marathon I couldn't find an app that simply told me what to run each day and synced cleanly to my Apple Watch — so I co-founded and designed RunPlan. I own the entire product design end-to-end and we ship fast as a two-person, AI-native team. I validated the MVP on my own marathon, then we launched publicly with 5K, 10K and half-marathon plans.

The problem

I started with a ChatGPT-generated training plan. Solid on paper, useless in practice — it was just text: it couldn't sync to my watch, couldn't track a workout, and couldn't tell me what today actually was.

So I tested the market — major brands and indie apps alike — and kept hitting the same three walls: forced sign-ups, unreliable Apple Watch sync, and interfaces that buried the one thing a runner needs: "what do I run today, and why?"

Research — competitive teardown

I mapped where each leading app breaks down at a specific point in the runner's real journey:

  • Nike Run Club — "Day 1 / Day 2" logic detached from a real calendar. Constant cognitive load: "It's Wednesday — am I on Day 3 or 4? Did I miss yesterday or was it a rest day?"
  • Runkeeper — login friction and aggressive paywalls; plan structure and basic analytics locked behind premium; frequent watch-sync failures.
  • Runna — genuinely good features, but ~€20/mo for an ecosystem (coaches, community, events) most runners don't want.
  • Apple Workouts — accurate, stable, effortless to track — but it only tracks. No structure, no plan, no "why".

The insight: the market was split between trackers with no brain (Apple) and platforms with too much overhead (everyone else). Nobody was simply the structured plan that lives on your wrist.

Goals & principles

  • Kill decision fatigue — make "what do I run today" a one-glance answer.
  • Watch-first — the plan should live where you start the run.
  • Privacy-first — no accounts, no ads, data stays on the device.
  • Single-purpose — a tool, not another ecosystem demanding attention.

Key design decisions

I framed RunPlan around a few decisions, each tied to a real behavioural problem — not a feature list.

1. Anchor the habit to the calendar, not to "Day N"

Problem: abstract day-numbering forces a daily negotiation and leaks adherence.
Decision: workouts are anchored to real weekdays — "Tuesday = intervals".
Why: it converts a high-effort daily choice into an automatic habit — you stop deciding and start preparing.
Trade-off: less "do any day whenever" flexibility — a deliberate trade for habit formation.

2. Make the "why" visible to keep people in the plan

Problem: runners quit mid-plan when a workout feels arbitrary.
Decision: expose the macro-structure — Foundation → Strength → Speed → Taper — and show exactly where you are.
Why: understanding the arc builds competence and autonomy, which is what actually drives adherence.

3. Bake training science in, so the user doesn't have to know it

Problem: beginners assume "more hard running = faster" and overtrain.
Decision: automatic periodization — a 3-week build followed by an intentional recovery week (methodology grounded in Daniels, Higdon, Pfitzinger).
Trade-off: sometimes the app tells a motivated runner to ease off — counter-intuitive, but correct.

4. Be the brain for Apple Watch — augment, don't replace

Problem: apps that replace Apple's tracking sync badly and duplicate data.
Decision: RunPlan owns the plan and structure; tracking hands off to Apple Fitness, no duplicate data.
Why: don't fight the platform standard runners already trust — sit on top of it.

What I deliberately left out: no social feed, no coaching community, no events marketplace, no accounts, no ads. Scoping out was as much of the product work as scoping in.
