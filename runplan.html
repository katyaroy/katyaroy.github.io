Why I built RunPlan

As an Apple Watch user training for distance, I wanted a structured, calendar-based plan that lived on my wrist — telling me what to run today, and why. Nothing did quite that:

  • Apple's own apps tracked my runs but gave me no plan, no structure.
  • Runna was the closest — structured plans, done well — but it's app-first and heavier than I wanted; I wanted the plan to live natively on the watch, calendar-first.
  • Garmin had the training structure, but in the wrong ecosystem for me. I was already on Apple Watch — and Garmin's plan setup was more complex than I expected, with a clunky phone-to-watch hand-off: you download a plan, then install it separately, with no smooth connection between app and watch.

So the gap was specific: Garmin-grade structure with Apple-native smoothness. RunPlan is my bet on it — a periodized plan that sits on a real calendar, syncs seamlessly to the watch, and starts the right workout with one tap.

Research — competitive teardown

Before building, I mapped where each leading app breaks down at a specific point in the runner's real journey. They failed at the same three walls: forced sign-ups, unreliable Apple Watch sync, and interfaces that bury the one thing a runner needs — "what do I run today, and why?"

  • Nike Run Club — "Day 1 / Day 2" logic detached from a real calendar. Constant cognitive load: "It's Wednesday — am I on Day 3 or 4? Did I miss yesterday or was it a rest day?"
  • Runkeeper — login friction and aggressive paywalls; plan structure and basic analytics locked behind premium; frequent watch-sync failures.
  • Runna — genuinely good features, but ~€20/mo for an ecosystem (coaches, community, events) most runners don't want.
  • Apple Workouts — accurate, stable, effortless to track — but it only tracks. No structure, no plan, no "why".

The insight: the market was split between trackers with no brain (Apple) and platforms with too much overhead (everyone else). Nobody was simply the structured plan that lives on your wrist.

Talking to runners

Alongside the teardown, I ran lightweight sessions with runner friends and informal problem interviews — I'd hand people the MVP and watch them try to build a plan, and, more tellingly, try to grasp why they'd want one. A few things came back consistently:

  • Setup and comprehension were the real barrier — people struggled to see why a plan mattered and how to start one. This is exactly why I later prioritised simplifying onboarding over polishing deeper screens.
  • Existing plans feel complex — which reinforced the core principle: hide the training science, surface one clear answer.
  • People love ticking off a completed workout — so I leaned into completion and visible progress as a motivator, not an afterthought.

Goals & principles

  • Kill decision fatigue — make "what do I run today" a one-glance answer.
  • Watch-first — the plan should live where you start the run.
  • Privacy-first — no accounts, no ads, data stays on the device.
  • Single-purpose — a tool, not another ecosystem demanding attention.

Key design decisions

I framed RunPlan around a few decisions, each tied to a real behavioural problem — not a feature list.

1. Anchor the habit to the calendar, not to "Day N"

Problem: abstract day-numbering forces a daily negotiation and leaks adherence.
Decision: workouts are anchored to real weekdays — "Tuesday = intervals".
Why: it converts a high-effort daily choice into an automatic habit — you stop deciding and start preparing.
Trade-off: less "do any day whenever" flexibility — a deliberate trade for habit formation.

2. Make the "why" visible to keep people in the plan

Problem: runners quit mid-plan when a workout feels arbitrary.
Decision: expose the macro-structure — Foundation → Strength → Speed → Taper — and show exactly where you are.
Why: understanding the arc builds competence and autonomy, which is what actually drives adherence.

3. Bake training science in, so the user doesn't have to know it

Problem: beginners assume "more hard running = faster" and overtrain.
Decision: automatic periodization — a 3-week build followed by an intentional recovery week (methodology grounded in Daniels, Higdon, Pfitzinger).
Trade-off: sometimes the app tells a motivated runner to ease off — counter-intuitive, but correct.

4. Be the brain for Apple Watch — augment, don't replace

Problem: apps that replace Apple's tracking sync badly and duplicate data.
Decision: RunPlan owns the plan and structure; tracking hands off to Apple Fitness, no duplicate data.
Why: don't fight the platform standard runners already trust — sit on top of it.

What I deliberately left out: no social feed, no coaching community, no events marketplace, no accounts, no ads. Scoping out was as much of the product work as scoping in.

Designing for the wrist (watchOS)

The watch is where the run actually starts, so it drove the core interaction design. And the hardest, most important problem in the whole product was exactly the thing that made me start it: a truly seamless Apple Watch ↔ iPhone connection, so the plan, the live run and your progress always agree. Getting that invisible plumbing right is what makes RunPlan feel effortless on the wrist — and it's the difference I couldn't find anywhere else.

How we ship — two people, AI-native

RunPlan is built by two. My co-founder, an engineer, owns the plan-generation algorithms and the heavy logic. I own the product surface — research, UX/UI and the marketing site — plus App Store distribution and Firebase analytics, and I increasingly take design changes straight into production code with AI (Claude Code, Gemini and ChatGPT, from the terminal). When iOS 26 introduced Liquid Glass, I re-designed the whole app to it directly in code.

We release roughly once a month, sometimes more, and we re-prioritise by impact rather than a fixed roadmap. For example, we simplified onboarding before polishing the activity view — a smoother first run is a bigger lever on the experience than a nicer screen deeper in. We also ship technical fixes fast as we learn what doesn't hold up: our watch–iPhone sync was async-only, so a finished workout didn't update immediately — we added a new sync path to make it reliable. And because it's a running app, I test it the only way that's honest — on simulators and out on real runs, checking that average pace and mileage compute correctly and that interval guidance is clear and easy to follow on the wrist; that's how we've caught a long tail of bugs. No design-to-dev handoff means a two-person team ships like a bigger one.

Outcome

RunPlan is live on the App Store — 5K, 10K and half-marathon plans, no sign-ups, no ads — and growing entirely organically; we haven't spent anything on marketing yet. Volumes are early-stage, but the signal I watch is store conversion: roughly 9% of people who reach the product page install the app, comfortably above the low-single-digit rate most apps see. Since I designed the store presence and the product, that's the number that tells me the positioning lands. The product is built; the honest next phase is distribution.

What I learned

  • A "simple" idea is rarely simple. "A calendar of runs" sounded trivial — but making the plan flexible and varied took a genuinely sophisticated generation engine underneath.
  • The hardest problems are invisible. The Apple Watch ↔ iPhone ecosystem is intricate, and reliable sync between them was far more work than any screen — yet it's exactly what makes the product feel effortless.
  • AI changed our slope. Once I started shipping code with AI alongside design, our ideas scaled and we moved dramatically faster — two people operating well above their size.
  • Built isn't adopted. The product was the easier half; getting it in front of runners is its own discipline — and the thing I most want to crack next.

What's next

The roadmap is big and I see real room to grow: new plan types (shipping soon), richer runner analytics and progress, and an engagement layer — streaks, a monthly-recap "stories" feature, and more — to turn a training plan into a habit people come back to.
