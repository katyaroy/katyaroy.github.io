Autoship — subscription delivery, new to the market

Introduced recurring scheduled delivery to a large pet e-commerce platform — a behaviour the market had never seen — without confusing a single shopper.

Overview

The company wanted to launch "a completely new way of ordering" — automatic, scheduled recurring delivery (set frequency, weekday and time). Subscription ordering didn't exist in this market yet, so user education was as important as the interface. I designed the feature end-to-end; orders and average order value rose, and it triggered a broader checkout redesign.

The problem

Launching recurring delivery wasn't a feature problem — it was a behaviour problem. The biggest risk was confusing customers with something they'd never seen. Business goals: save customers time, attract new customers, and increase loyalty.

Research & discovery

  • Interviewed customers and call-center staff about expectations and real pain points.
  • Analysed foreign competitors who'd already made subscription work — with the explicit caution that "what worked abroad may well not work here."
  • Corridor-tested prototypes to check the purchase flow and the new terminology read clearly.

The core challenge

"The hardest part was not to confuse the user — not to clutter the interface with unfamiliar words and new CTAs." Every decision below serves that one constraint.

Key design decisions

1. Opt-in, layered on top of normal shopping

Decision: add to cart as usual, then optionally turn on Autoship — not all products suit recurring purchase.
Why: introduce the new model without forcing it or breaking the familiar flow.
Trade-off: lower visibility than forcing it — accepted to protect clarity and trust.

2. Sell it where the decision happens — the product card

Decision: show price comparison and benefits inline, with immediate setup. Logged-in users get an Autoship suggestion; others are prompted to log in.
Why: explain the value at the moment of intent, not in a separate funnel.

3. A checkout that reassures

Decision: a distinct checkout header that signals security without distraction; Autoship limited to the store's own delivery (operational control), while regular orders kept all methods.
Trade-off: less delivery flexibility on subscriptions — a deliberate trade for reliability.

4. Easy exit = trust to commit

Decision: change, cancel the whole order, skip the next delivery, or restart anytime; manage individual deliveries.
Why: people only commit to recurring billing if leaving is effortless.

5. Educate across every touchpoint

Decision: awareness through banner, slider, product cards and cart, plus a dedicated support page explaining Autoship "in simple language".

An honest limitation

The first version required manual call-center confirmation for every Autoship order — time-intensive, and the calls annoyed users. I flagged it as the priority next iteration.

Outcome

  • Orders and average order value increased significantly; adoption was strongest in major cities.
  • The improved checkout reduced cart abandonment from the previously complicated flow.
  • Autoship catalysed a broader cart, checkout, navigation and login redesign — impact well beyond the original feature.
